A variable is a name defined in a makefile to represent a string of text, called the variable’s value.

Variables and functions in all parts of a makefile are expanded when read, except when:

  • in recipes
  • in the right-hand sides of variable definitions using = or ?=
  • in right-hand sides of +=: deferred or immediate, depending on its left hand
  • in the bodies of variable definitions using the define directive: may be immediate when in the body of `define immediate +=`, else deferred
  • 3.7 How make Reads a Makefile has some further details

The value a variable expands to is that of its most recent definition at the time of expansion. In other words, variables are dynamically scoped.

A variable name may be any sequence of characters not containing ‘:’, ‘#’, ‘=’, or whitespace. variable names containing characters other than letters, numbers, and underscores should be considered carefully; Variable names beginning with ‘.’ and an uppercase letter may be given special meaning in future versions of make; we recommend using lower case letters for variable names that serve internal purposes in the makefile.

we can refer a variable as ${multi-chars-var}, $(multi-chars-var) or $single-char-var (such as $@); to get a literal $ in makefile, we use $$.

keep in mind IN SHELL:

  • \$ is used to get a literal $
  • $$ is to get the shell's PID
  • $(pwd) is to call the prog named pwd(while in Makefile, it's referring the var named pwd); so in recipe, you write $$(pwd) to get result from the pwd util
  • in shell, $PATH is to get val-of-env-var PATH; while in Makefile, $PATH is to get val-of-var P then append text "ATH"
